h1>Ski Jumping

Ski jumping is a winter sport in which competitors aim to achieve the longest jump after descending from a specially designed ramp on their skis.

The sport traces its origins back to Norway in the late 19th century, and has been included in the Winter Olympics since 1924.

There are several different ski jumping events, including the individual normal hill, individual large hill, and team event.

Ski Jumping Techniques

Ski jumping techniques have evolved over the years, with modern jumpers using a V-style technique for maximum distance. This involves keeping the skis together and pointing them downwards at a slight angle to generate lift.

Jumpers also use a Telemark landing, where the back ski is lifted off the ground and the front ski is used for landing.

Ski Jumping Equipment

Ski jumpers use specialized equipment, including skis, boots, bindings, and a ski suit. The skis used in ski jumping are longer and narrower than those used in cross-country skiing.

Jumpers also use poles, which are longer than those used in cross-country skiing and have a special basket at the bottom for take-off.

Ski Jumping Venues

Ski jumping competitions are held at specially constructed venues called ski jumping hills. These hills are made of snow and ice and have a steep incline leading up to the take-off ramp.

Some of the most famous ski jumping venues include the Holmenkollen Ski Jump in Norway, the ski jumping hill in Planica, Slovenia and the ski jumping hill in Oberstdorf, Germany.
